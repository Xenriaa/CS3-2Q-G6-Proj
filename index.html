The Problem

"Homelessness" is defined as a condition wherein an individual or family lacks a fixed night-time residence; an individual or family lives in a public or private facility that is not designed for, or ordinarily used as, a regular sleeping accommodation for human beings; an individual or family lives in a shelter that is designed and operated to provide temporary living accommodations; or an individual or family has a place of residence at the end of the night, but lacks a fixed nighttime or permanent residence and is actually and intermittently residing at the place, lacking only the resources and support that would allow the individual or family to obtain a permanent residence.

The Costs and Harms of Homelessness
Homelessness reflects and exacerbates significant harms to individuals, communities, and society. These impacts are considerable and often difficult to measure, including social, medical, behavioral, economic, and ethical costs. While discussing these costs may seem like "homelessness economics," understanding the damage caused and the benefits of effective solutions can create urgency for addressing the issue.

We emphasize the inherent value of ending homelessness, arguing that the moral responsibility to tackle it goes beyond any cost-benefit analysis. Recognizing what is at stake can drive action, and our goal is to provide insights that strengthen the case for solving homelessness.
